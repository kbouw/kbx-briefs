## Week of February 23 - March 1, 2026

### Executive Summary

The AI industry experienced a dramatic politicization this week as military partnerships split OpenAI and Anthropic into opposing camps, with OpenAI embracing Department of War contracts while Anthropic faced government designation as a "supply chain risk." Simultaneously, coding agents crossed a critical capability threshold that's fundamentally disrupting software development workflows, forcing the industry to grapple with new engineering patterns and economic realities.

### Top Trends

**AI Military-Industrial Complex Formation**
- Evidence: OpenAI announced formal Department of War agreements for classified network deployment; Anthropic faced government designation as supply chain risk; Google employees pushed for military AI "red lines" 
- Why it matters: The AI industry is splitting along geopolitical lines, with commercial partnerships becoming national security issues that could fragment global AI development and access

**Coding Agents Cross Capability Threshold**
- Evidence: Andrej Karpathy reported agents went from "basically non-functional to genuinely disruptive" in December 2024; Max Woolf ported entire Python libraries to Rust; multiple developers demonstrated month-long projects completed correctly
- Why it matters: This represents an inflection point where AI coding assistance transitions from novelty to core development workflow, fundamentally changing software economics and engineering practices

**Open Source Defensive Strategies**
- Evidence: tldraw moved test suites to private repos after Cloudflare's AI-powered Next.js port; Anthropic offered free Claude Max to OSS maintainers; new funding initiatives for open source emerged
- Why it matters: Open source projects face existential questions about protecting IP from AI replication while maintaining community benefits, forcing new economic models

**Memory and Hardware Supply Constraints**
- Evidence: RAM represents 35% of HP PC costs; smartphone market forecast to decline due to memory shortages; ASML announced 50% chip yield improvements needed by 2030
- Why it matters: Hardware bottlenecks are becoming primary constraints on both AI deployment and consumer electronics, potentially reshaping product strategies

**Government Tech Sovereignty Push**
- Evidence: Denmark abandoning Microsoft software; US diplomats fighting data sovereignty; California requiring age verification in operating systems
- Why it matters: National governments are actively decoupling from US tech dominance, creating fragmented regulatory landscapes that complicate global product strategies

### Developing Stories

**AI Safety Governance Collapse**: Anthropic quietly dropped its Responsible Scaling Policy while facing government pressure, suggesting safety commitments are yielding to commercial and political pressures across the industry.

**Agentic Engineering Patterns**: New professional practices emerging around coding agents, including "red/green TDD" prompting, "First run the tests" workflows, and treating code as "cheap to generate" - indicating systematic approaches to human-AI collaboration.

**Browser Engine Renaissance**: Both Ladybird's Rust migration (assisted by AI) and Firefox's AI kill switch feature suggest renewed competition and differentiation in browser technology.
